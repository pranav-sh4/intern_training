TCP/IP

The robust protocol suite that standardized how data moves across heterogeneous networks.

The journey of the internet began in the late 1960s with the creation of ARPANET, funded by the U.S. Department of Defense. It was the first network to implement the protocol suite TCP/IP, laying the groundwork for everything we see today.

lightbulb Did You Know?

The very first message sent over ARPANET was "LO". The system crashed as Charley Kline tried to type "LOGIN".

This early network demonstrated that packet switching was more efficient than traditional circuit switching. It emphasized decentralized communication, ensuring that the network could survive even if parts of it were destroyed.

hub The Packet Switching Revolution

In the early 1960s, independent of each other, Paul Baran in the US and Donald Davies in the UK developed the concept of distributed communications. Baran's work at the RAND Corporation envisioned a network that could survive a nuclear strike.

Rather than a centralized exchange, Baran proposed breaking messages into small "blocks" (later called packets). This was radical: it meant no single point of failure and higher utilization of existing lines.

open_in_new The Browser Wars & Standards

The mid-90s saw a fierce competition for dominance over how people accessed the web. Netscape Navigator, the early leader, faced a massive threat when Microsoft began bundling Internet Explorer with Windows.

This "war" drove the rapid development of web standards. Features we take for granted today—like cookies, JavaScript, and CSS—were often weaponized to gain a competitive edge.

"We are going to cut off their air supply. Everything they're selling, we're going to give away for free."
— Microsoft Executive on Netscape

gavel The Standards Body Era & W3C

As the conflict intensified, the web risked total fragmentation. The emergence of the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) marked a turning point toward the "Standards Body Era." Before consensus was reached, proprietary extensions reigned supreme.

A prime example was scripting: Netscape's JavaScript and Microsoft's JScript. Developers were forced to write separate codebases until ECMAScript standardization brought harmony.

install_mobile Mobile Web Revolution

The introduction of the iPhone in 2007 and the subsequent rollout of 4G networks fundamentally changed the Internet's traffic patterns. For the first time, users expected a desktop-class experience on a 3.5-inch screen.

This shift led to the "Mobile First" design philosophy. It wasn't just about screen size; it was about context. Location-based services (GPS) and persistent connectivity birthed the App Economy, moving the web from a place you "go to" to a service that "follows you."

4g_mobiledata smartphone

The "Full Web" in your pocket

Before 2007, the mobile web was WAP—text-heavy and restricted. The iPhone introduced the Mobile Safari browser, forcing the web to become responsive.

aspect_ratio The Rise of Responsive Design

In 2010, Ethan Marcotte published a seminal article titled "Responsive Web Design." Before this, companies often maintained separate "m.sites"—entirely different websites for mobile users that were difficult to sync and maintain.

Marcotte's approach utilized fluid grids, flexible images, and media queries to create a unified codebase that adapted to any screen. This shift ended the era of device-specific forks and paved the way for the modern, accessible web we use today.

dynamic_form Web 2.0 and Beyond

The early 2000s marked the transition from "Web 1.0"—a collection of static read-only pages—to Web 2.0, defined by interactivity and user-generated content. This wasn't a technical upgrade of the internet itself, but a shift in how it was used.

Technologies like AJAX (Asynchronous JavaScript and XML) allowed pages to update without refreshing, enabling the rise of rich web applications and SaaS (Software as a Service). Suddenly, the browser became a platform for complex tools, not just a document viewer.

This era birthed the Social Media giants, turning every user into a creator. The web evolved into a global conversation redefining our daily digital interactions and the global economy.
